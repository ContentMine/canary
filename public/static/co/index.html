Content mining and Hargreaves

Content mining means processing information in search of new information. It is often called text and data mining (TDM), but we also process figures and images, so we believe content mining is more appropriate. To start content mining, some source material is required. Any digital document would do, but there is an issue of copyright - often, automated processing of documents is restricted by copyright.

However in 2014 the UK introduced an exception to copyright law; the Hargreaves exception stipulates that anyone in the UK with the right to read a document also has the right to mine it - regardless of where the document is from and also regardless of whether there are any copyright restrictions on the document. The exception overrides them.

So, in the UK, we can content mine a lot - and we can share any factual output with anyone, because facts are not copyrightable. We can also do this on behalf of our research collaborators.

From this and previous projects we have gathered a lot of expert knowledge about the copyright exception, and developed software tools and processes for responsible, legal retrieval, normalisation, and content mining.

ContentMine began when Peter Murray-Rust, a scientist and advocate for open science, sought to use the Hargreaves copyright exception to mine the scientific literature as a form of new research.

So Peter started the ContentMine project, and began demonstrating the usefulness of content mining, and providing tools and services to others.

The Shuttleworth Foundation believed that Peter could change the world, and have funded him since 2014 as a Shuttleworth Fellow to lead the ContentMine project.

Peter has put together a great team of people and they are guided by an excellent advisory board.
